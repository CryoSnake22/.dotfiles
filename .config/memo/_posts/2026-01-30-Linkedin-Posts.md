# Mon 2 Feb 2026 11:08:11 EST

I looked at my project today and thought:
"What the hell am I doing?"

It was just a jumbled mess of features.
Job scraping.
Skill comparisons.
Analytics dashboards.
Progress tracking.

And I couldn't remember why any of it mattered.

So I asked myself:
"Wait a second. What was I even trying to solve in the first place?"

I had to actually stop and think about it.

And this is the uncomfortable truth that hit me.

I'd spent so much time building.
Pouring everything into it.
Each feature felt important.
Each addition felt like progress.

But somewhere along the way, I got lost.

You start with this clear vision of the magic you want to create.
Then you start building.
And building.
And adding "just one more thing."

Until one day you look up and realize:
You've built a lot.
But you can't explain what value it provides in one sentence.

This is the trap I'm slowly beginning to understand:

More features feels like more value.
But features without clarity typically just lead to an unclear value proposition.

The magic isn't in how much you build.
It's in how clearly you solve the one thing that matters.

I wanted to help people understand where their skills are lacking and how to actually show what they have.

Not just "you're missing X."
Not just "go learn Y."
Understand the lack, fix it, then show your skills. That was the magic.

Everything else?
Just features I convinced myself were necessary.

So I'm stripping it all back.
Back to the original problem.
Back to the magic.

# Sun 1 Feb 2026 13:17:27 EST

I just cut my AI costs by 3x AND got better results by questioning one default setting.

Most developers accept whatever model their AI tools suggest.
GPT-4, Claude Opus, the premium options.
It feels safer. More capable. Less likely to break.

I did the same thing.

But then I actually read the docs.

Turns out I'd been using GPT-4o-mini ($0.15 input / $0.075 output) when GPT-5-nano ($0.05 input / $0.005 output) was both cheaper AND better for my specific tasks.

3x cost reduction on input.
15x on output.
And it performs better for what I'm actually building.

The AI completely missed this.

It didn't optimize for my use case.
It didn't optimize for my budget.
It just defaulted to "safe."

And "safe" meant paying more for worse results.

Here's what most people don't realize:

- AI tools suggest models based on maximum capability
- Not based on what your specific task actually needs
- Not based on performance for your use case
- And definitely not based on cost efficiency
- You're paying premium for a model that might not even be the best fit

So now I do this:

- Check the docs before accepting defaults
- Test multiple models for my specific use case
- Choose based on performance AND cost
- Only pay premium when it actually performs better

Not blind trust. Actual comparison.

Spending 10 minutes reading docs instead of trusting AI defaults saved me hundreds in waste and got me better performance.

If you're using AI coding tools, question the defaults. The "recommended" model might be both more expensive and worse for what you're building.

A lot of people won’t say this out loud, but walking into a hackathon can be super intimidating.

Everyone looks smarter than you.
More experienced.
More confident.
You see all the cool projects people are building and start wondering if you even belong there.

That was me last weekend.

I went in with a pretty big inferiority complex.
We started building and I kept thinking, “Man, everyone else here knows so much more than me.”

But once we actually got into the project… something shifted.

We started brainstorming.
Ruthlessly cutting down inefficient ideas because the time constraint was brutal.
Moving fast because we had no choice.

And somewhere in that chaos, I forgot to be insecure.
I forgot to compare myself.
I was just… building.
And I fell in love with it.

We didn’t win.
And yeah, it stung for a moment.

But the thing that surprised me most was that losing wasn’t the part that stayed with me.

What stuck was the feeling of creating something from scratch with people I care about.

Those 36 hours of building like crazy and sleeping on the floor lit a fire in me.

I want to build more.
I want to learn more.
I want to become someone who actually shows up and creates things.

If you’ve had a similar moment at a hackathon, I’d love to hear your story.
